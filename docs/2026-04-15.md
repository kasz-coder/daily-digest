**No Priors — "AI for Atoms: How Periodic Labs is Revolutionizing Materials Engineering"**

The Takeaway: A former OpenAI VP of post-training is building AI systems that connect to the physical world—discovering new materials through closed-loop experimentation—and sees this as the next frontier after language models transformed software.

Liam Fedus, co-creator of ChatGPT and former VP of post-training at OpenAI, is now building Periodic Labs—an AI foundation lab for atoms. Coming from a physics background (dark matter research), he sees a wave of physicists moving into AI because "it's such an incredible field where you have such high leverage in computer science."

On why materials science matters now: Language models transformed how we interact with information, but "you're not going to see the same kind of acceleration in science and technology unless you start connecting these things to the physical world." The key enabling factors were improved reasoning, test-time inference, reliable error correction, and the rise of coding agents—foundational technologies necessary to connect AI to physical experiments.

On data: They leverage order tens of trillions of tokens from open-source models for foundational understanding, but that's insufficient. The real power comes from experimental data—grounding in reality. "It's not just a pool of data, it's this interactive closed loop system that is so powerful."

On architecture: Language models serve as an orchestration layer—a copilot that can direct experiments, ingesting literature and experimental data, while orchestrating specialized neural nets designed for atomic systems with symmetry awareness.

On the vision: "We are trying to give humanity this agency for atomic rearrangement synthesis, and we think it's going to just be a huge accelerator. If our physical world could keep up at some fraction to our digital world, I think life will just feel dramatically different."

On robotics: While advanced robotics would be a huge accelerator, Periodic doesn't require it yet. They currently use people and reliable autonomous systems to produce high-quality data. The goal is generating diverse, high-quality experimental data—automation is just assistance to that.
