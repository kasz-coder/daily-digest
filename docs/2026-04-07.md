**Aaron Levie** (CEO at Box) argues that AI agents making things more efficient will induce demand for more of that skill, rather than eliminating work. "This is why the AI jobs predictions will not play out as advertised." AI making it easy to produce more code means we'll apply code to far more parts of our businesses—marketing automation, client onboarding, modernizing old systems, more research on existing data. More software means vastly more security risks, leading to more security roles. AI lowers the cost of previously niche categories—companies will do 10X more with video and graphics, needing more media people. More legal challenges mean more lawyers. The examples are endless once you think through second-order effects of agents making work more efficient.

## Podcasts

**Latent Space** — "Moonlake: Causal World Models should be Multimodal, Interactive, and Efficient — with Chris Manning and Fan-yun Sun"

Chris Manning and Fan-yun Sun from Moonlake discuss why causal world models should be multimodal, interactive, and efficient. The conversation explores the challenges of building world models that can understand and predict physical realities, emphasizing the need for multimodal approaches that go beyond text. They argue that world models must be interactive—able to query and manipulate their environment—rather than passive predictors. Efficiency is critical: models that can only run massive compute can't scale to real-world deployment. The takeaway: the next generation of AI needs to move beyond passive text prediction toward active, efficient agents that can truly understand and interact with the physical world.
