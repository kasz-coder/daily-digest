**Andrej Karpathy**, former Director of AI at Tesla and founding team at OpenAI, shared thoughts on "personalized AI" through a personal wiki approach. He highlighted four key advantages: the memory is explicit and navigable, your data stays on your local computer (not in AI provider systems), it's built on universal file formats making it interoperable with any tools, and you can use whatever AI you want over it. He argues this puts users in full control. He also expressed bullishness on AI empowering people to increase government visibility, legibility, and accountability — noting that while governments have historically made society legible, AI now enables society to do this in reverse. Examples include tracking spending bills, diff tracking legislation, lobbying influence graphs, and local government decisions.

**Box CEO Aaron Levie** argued that the context layer will always be the core part of the AI stack for enterprise use cases. He explained that unlike coding where developers can access most relevant information, enterprise teams have entirely different access levels to corporate knowledge — sanitizing this is hard and having models keep secrets is impossible. He also noted that as AI models improve at tool handling and context windows get bigger without degradation, agents can now be designed more like how people work rather than needing workarounds for model limitations. This enables qualitatively different use cases, which is how Box redesigned their AI architecture.

## PODCASTS

**No Priors** — "AI for Atoms: How Periodic Labs is Revolutionizing Materials Engineering with Co-Founder Liam Fedus"

Liam Fedus, former VP of post-training at OpenAI and co-creator of ChatGPT, is now building Periodic Labs — an AI foundation lab for atoms. His vision: connecting AI systems to the physical world to revolutionize materials science and chemistry.

The Takeaway: AI that can understand and generate matter could accelerate physical world development at a pace similar to how software engineering has transformed — but atoms are harder.

Fedus has a physics background (undergrad research on dark matter), and he's not alone — many physicists are moving into AI because the field offers high leverage to make real impact. At Google Brain in 2016-2017, he worked on early innovations including distributed training strategies, mixture of experts, and the transformer.

The core insight: "Science ultimately isn't sitting in a room thinking really hard. You have to conduct experiments. You have to learn from them. You have to interface with reality." His prior work on ChatGPT in late 2022 was exciting but too weak for physical world applications. As reasoning improved and test-time inference became viable, the technology became ready for connecting to atoms.

On data: Periodic uses order tens of trillions of tokens from open source models as a foundation, but this is insufficient alone — literature data spans orders of magnitude in reported values, making ground truth elusive. Experimental data provides grounding through an interactive closed loop system.

The architecture uses language models as an orchestration layer — they direct experiments, ingest literature, and coordinate specialized neural nets designed for atomic systems with symmetry awareness. The vision: "give humanity this agency for atomic rearrangement synthesis" — if the physical world could keep up at some fraction of the pace of the digital world, "life will just feel dramatically different."
